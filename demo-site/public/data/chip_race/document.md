# The Global AI Chip Race: US-China Technology Competition

Source: Compiled from Semafor, IEEE Spectrum, Time, South China Morning Post (December 2025 - January 2026)

The global competition for AI chip supremacy between the United States and China has intensified dramatically, reshaping the semiconductor industry and international trade relations.

## Trump's Chip Export Decision

In a major policy shift, President Donald Trump approved Nvidia's sales of H200 AI chips to China, jolting the technology trade war between the two superpowers. Jensen Huang, CEO of Nvidia, had been lobbying for broader access to the Chinese market. White House spokesman Kush Desai defended the decision as keeping Chinese firms dependent on Nvidia's ecosystem rather than developing homegrown alternatives.

Gregory Allen, senior adviser at the Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), argued that limited exports could slow China's development of independent chip architectures. Former Trump chief strategist Steve Bannon criticized the move as compromising national security.

## China's Response: Building Self-Sufficiency

Huawei has matured its Ascend line of AI chips under US sanctions. In September 2025, Huawei laid out a multiyear public roadmap with the Ascend 950 expected in 2026. Despite progress, estimates put China's leading-edge AI chip production at just 3 percent of US totals, and public data suggests top US AI chips are five times more powerful than China's best.

Chinese hyperscalers including ByteDance announced aggressive AI capital expenditure plans, pursuing more compute-efficient model strategies in response to US export restrictions. DeepSeek, a Chinese AI company, demonstrated rapid AI development by creating advanced models at lower compute costs, challenging assumptions about the necessity of cutting-edge hardware.

Alibaba and other Chinese tech giants have been prohibited from acquiring advanced US-made AI chips, forcing them to develop alternative approaches. The Chinese Communist Party's recommendations for the 15th Five-Year Plan include making forward-looking plans for industries of the future including AI, quantum technology, and brain-computer interfaces.

## The Global Supply Chain

The semiconductor industry remains geographically concentrated in East Asia. TSMC in Taiwan manufactures the most advanced chips, using extreme ultraviolet lithography machines made exclusively by ASML in the Netherlands. Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan lead in semiconductor manufacturing, while the United States and the European Union hold significant stakes in chip design and equipment.

Semiconductor onshoring gathered pace amid record global investment in chipmaking equipment, with China leading in overall investment volume. Intel, Samsung, and TSMC are all building new fabrication facilities in the United States, supported by the CHIPS and Science Act funding.

## AI Development Competition

The AI development race between the two countries shows contrasting strengths. The United States leads in core technology development, AI frameworks, cloud infrastructure, and quantum computing. China leads or is closing the gap in practical physical AI and robotics deployments, advancing technological self-sufficiency through aggressive industrial policy.

OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and Meta AI lead American AI research, while Baidu, Alibaba DAMO Academy, Tencent AI Lab, and ByteDance's research division drive Chinese innovation. The competition extends beyond chips to models, data, talent, and applications.

Technology analyst Ben Thompson noted that the current approach mirrors decades-old sliding scale technology export policies that attempt to balance maintaining dominance with remaining competitive in global markets. The Department of Justice has increased scrutiny of technology transfers and intellectual property protections.
