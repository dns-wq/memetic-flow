# Six AI Governance Priorities for 2026

Source: Partnership on AI (https://partnershiponai.org/resource/six-ai-governance-priorities/)
Published: February 11, 2026
Authors: Stephanie Ifayemi, Jacob Pratt

2026 is a critical year for policy. With India's AI Impact Summit, the EU's Code of Practice on AI content labelling, the first UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance, and the G7 summit in the first few months alone, policymakers face a crowded calendar alongside US repositioning and geopolitical uncertainty. These forums will shape key decisions, including who gets access to AI compute and infrastructure, how synthetic content is labelled amid growing pressure for legislative action particularly around child safety, and whether capacity building narrows or widens the digital divide.

The stakes are high and the case for action is clear. AI is already accelerating understanding of brain diseases, transforming learning and reshaping workflows. But 2025 also gave warnings: AI therapists operating without guardrails, private conversations made public, and agentic tools wiping entire databases, twice. The gap between what AI can do and what governance can meaningfully oversee is widening.

With the help of the Policy Steering Committee, which was established to identify key questions that inform effective policymaking, six key priorities for AI governance in 2026 were identified. Realizing these goals requires a multistakeholder approach including elected officials, government workers, civil society, academia, and industry.

## Priority 1: Establish Foundational Infrastructure to Govern AI Agents

Agentic systems introduce new challenges, including potential non-reversibility of actions, open-ended decision-making pathways, and privacy vulnerabilities from expanded data access. Current infrastructure limitations around memory and context compound these risks and make oversight harder.

To enable responsible adoption in 2026, prioritizing evaluation frameworks that scale, accountability infrastructure for attribution and remediation, and assurance mechanisms that balance oversight with privacy is necessary.

Key actions include establishing transparency standards and secure protocols, including for inter-agent composition and merging properties. Driving forward protocols like MCP that are robust, secure, and fit for purpose. Clarifying the value chain by establishing an agreed upon value chain taxonomy to govern agents as integrated systems. Developing monitoring and oversight for accountability with privacy protections. Improving testing infrastructure by funding initiatives to improve evaluation validity and scalability.

## Priority 2: Strengthen Documentation and Public Reporting Mechanisms

There is still a lack of coherent strategies for how information should flow across the AI value chain. While the EU AI Act's transparency requirements and OECD made progress in 2025, efforts remain fragmented. Gaps remain in how documentation artifacts connect across the value chain as upstream developers document models without clarity on what downstream deployers need.

Key actions include coordinating documentation artifacts across the value-chain, standardizing templates tailored to system types and risks, and strengthening reporting frameworks that already have multistakeholder support such as The Hiroshima AI Process Framework to ensure interoperability.

## Priority 3: Coordinate Internationally Through Shared Baselines

As AI governance efforts multiply, the risk of fragmentation grows. Initiatives are proliferating at national, regional, and international levels without clear pathways toward convergence or mutual recognition.

Key actions include aligning on deployment challenges, creating a validated open evaluation repository, defining mutual recognition processes, and ensuring AI Summits like India's drive accountability with measurable outcomes. Interoperability between the International Network for Advanced AI Measurement, Evaluation and Science, and global initiatives such as the forthcoming Global South Network on AI Safety and Evaluations is essential.

## Priority 4: Preserve Human Voice and Epistemic Integrity

As AI increasingly mediates information, how authentic human voice is preserved must be explored. While AI offers unprecedented accessibility and personalization, it risks eroding the ability to discern generated content from human creation.

Key actions include balancing credentialing and detection tooling with efforts to build public literacy, protecting human-mediated ecosystems, reviewing media viability by exploring new economic models for media organizations, and examining the information access equity divide.

## Priority 5: Advance Public Understanding of AI and Workforce Resilience

Workforce readiness represents a critical policy frontier, but literacy must move beyond how to use tools toward assurance literacy: understanding when to rely on AI and how to evaluate its outputs.

Key actions include quantifying task-level capabilities, conducting workforce foresight, broadening AI adoption capacity, promoting assurance literacy, and uniting AI Policy and Economic Policy communities.

## Priority 6: Clarify AI Sovereignty Goals

The AI technology stack has evolved significantly since most national strategies were drafted. Policymakers now face difficult trade-offs: high infrastructure costs, complex strategic partnerships, and unavoidable dependencies on foreign providers.

Key actions include mapping AI supply chain dependencies, evaluating environmental tradeoffs of infrastructure needs, developing novel legal arrangements for shared digital public infrastructure, and ensuring public participation in governance decisions.
